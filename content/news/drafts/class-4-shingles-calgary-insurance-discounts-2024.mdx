## The 2024 Alberta Building Code Update: What It Means for Your Roof

On May 1, 2024, the [Government of Alberta](https://www.alberta.ca/building-codes-and-standards) officially brought the 2023 edition of the National Building Code into force. While this update covers a wide range of structural and energy standards, its timing is critical for homeowners in Southern Alberta. We're seeing a clear trend: as provincial standards move toward higher resiliency, the insurance industry is following suit with stricter requirements and better incentives for those who build beyond the bare minimum.

This regulatory shift didn't happen in a vacuum. According to the [Insurance Bureau of Canada (IBC)](https://www.ibc.ca/news-insights/news/severe-weather-causes-over-3-billion-in-insured-damage-in-2023), severe weather caused over $3.1 billion in insured damage across Canada in 2023. Alberta hail was a primary driver of these losses. For you, this means the cost of "doing nothing" is rising. Insurers are no longer just looking at the age of your roof; they're looking at its ability to survive the next storm without a total loss claim.

The new code environment encourages materials that can withstand the unique pressures of the Canadian climate. In Calgary, that pressure comes in the form of golf-ball-sized hail and rapid temperature swings. By choosing materials that align with these new resiliency goals, you aren't just following a code—you're positioning your home to be more "insurable" in a market that's becoming increasingly risk-averse.

## Will Class 4 Shingles Lower Your Calgary Insurance Premiums?

The short answer is: very likely, but you must have the right documentation. Insurers are reacting to the $3.1 billion in 2023 damages by incentivizing materials that reduce the frequency of claims. [AMA Insurance](https://ama.ab.ca/insurance/home/hail-resistance-roofing-discount), for example, explicitly offers a "Hail Resistance Roofing Discount" for homeowners who install impact-resistant products that meet the UL 2218 Class 4 standard.

To qualify for these credits, the shingles must pass a specific test where a 2-inch steel ball is dropped twice in the same spot without cracking the shingle's backing. This simulates the impact of heavy hail. When you install these on a [roof replacement](/services/roof-replacement), you're telling the insurer that your home is significantly less likely to require a $15,000 to $20,000 payout after a summer storm.

Policy terms vary by insurer. From what we've seen, these discounts can range from 5% to 15% off your annual premium, which adds up significantly over the 25-to-50-year lifespan of the roof.

## Why SBS-Modified Shingles are Built for "Hail Alley"

In Calgary, not all Class 4 shingles are created equal. You'll often hear us talk about **SBS-modified shingles Calgary** homeowners should prioritize. SBS stands for Styrene-Butadiene-Styrene, which is a type of synthetic rubber mixed into the asphalt. 

Standard shingles use "oxidized" asphalt, which is hard and becomes brittle as it ages. In contrast, SBS-modified shingles are "rubberized." This gives them two massive advantages in our climate:
1.  **Impact Recovery:** When hail hits an SBS shingle, the rubberized granules act like a shock absorber, bouncing back rather than cracking.
2.  **Thermal Flexibility:** Calgary's -30°C winter snaps make standard shingles as brittle as glass. From the roofs we see across Calgary, SBS shingles retain their flexibility even in extreme cold, meaning they are less likely to crack during high winds or when the house settles.

If you live in Northwest or Northeast Calgary—neighborhoods like Panorama Hills or Saddle Ridge—you're in the heart of "Hail Alley." In these zones, the material delta between a standard shingle and an SBS-modified Class 4 shingle isn't just an upgrade; it's a necessity for long-term peace of mind.

## The Financial Delta: Class 3 vs. Class 4 Upgrades

Many homeowners hesitate at the upfront cost of a Class 4 upgrade. It's true that the materials are more expensive than standard Class 3 shingles. However, when you factor in the potential for lower premiums and the likelihood of avoiding a high insurance deductible after a storm, the math changes.
